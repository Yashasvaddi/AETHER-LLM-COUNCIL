[Con-A] (CON):
India does not occupy China; the two nations merely share a contested frontier, not sovereign territory. Under the 1960 Sino‑Indian Agreement on the Panchsheel, both states recognized each other’s “integral territory” and pledged non‑interference. The Line of Actual Control (LAC) is a de‑facto boundary reflecting disputed claims, not a formal annexation—India administers only areas it historically controlled (e.g., Arunachal Pradesh), which China labels “South Tibet.” International law requires effective, continuous administration over a defined area; India lacks such control inside the People’s Republic of China, and no UN resolution or recognisable map delineates Indian occupation. Hence, the premise of “China occupied by India” is factually baseless.

[Con-B] (CON):
India does not occupy China. First, no Indian civil administration—no Indian government services, courts, or elections—exists inside internationally recognized Chinese territory. Lastly, diplomatic channels routinely discuss “border incidents,” not “occupation,” underscoring that Indian presence is limited to contested patrols, not a takeover of Chinese land.

Effective occupation, under international law, requires control over territory that another state lawfully owns. China’s claim rests solely on a pre‑1949 assertion never ratified by a treaty or UN resolution. Consequently, Indian civil services, infrastructure and troops operate within a region already accepted as Indian sovereign space, not inside the People’s Republic of China.
